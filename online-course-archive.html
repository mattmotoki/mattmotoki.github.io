Probabilistic Graphical Models Specialization ↑

  • Institution: Stanford University
  • Date Completed: November 11, 2017
  • Links: Course Website, Certificate of Achievement

Completed the three course specialization on probabilistic graphical models. The course was taught by Daphne Koller who is a Professor at Stanford University and an expert in the subject.

Probabilistic Graphical Models (PGMs) are a rich framework for encoding probability distributions over complex domains: joint (multivariate) distributions over large numbers of random variables that interact with each other. These representations sit at the intersection of statistics and computer science, relying on concepts from probability theory, graph algorithms, machine learning, and more. They are the basis for the state-of-the-art methods in a wide variety of applications, such as medical diagnosis, image understanding, speech recognition, natural language processing, and many, many more. They are also a foundational tool in formulating many machine learning problems.

The specialization consisted of three courses: Representation, Inference, and Learning.

Representation covered Bayesian Networks, which rely on a directed graph; and Markov networks, which use an undirected graph. The course discussed both the theoretical properties of these representations as well as their use in practice.

Inference addressed how a PGM can be used to answer questions. Even though a PGM generally describes a very high dimensional distribution, its structure is designed so as to allow questions to be answered efficiently. The course presented both exact and approximate algorithms for different types of inference tasks, and discusses where each could best be applied.

Learning addressed how a PGM can be learned from a data set of examples. The course discussed the key problems of parameter estimation in both directed and undirected models, as well as the structure learning task for directed models.

The Analytics Edge ↑

  • Institution: MITx
  • Date Completed: May 28, 2015
  • Links: Course Website, Certificate of Achievement, Kaggle Competition

This course was one of the first online courses that I ever took. It was taught by Dimitris Bertsimas who is well-known in the field of Operations Research. The course was primarily business analytics covering fundamental methods such as linear regression, logistic regression, CART, clustering, data visualization, and linear programming. The problem sets were done in R and Excel (linear programming solver). The final course project was to participate in a Kaggle competition; that was my first competition and it started my love for Kaggle.

Scalable Machine Learning ↑

  • Institution: BerkeleyX
  • Date Completed: August 06, 2015
  • Links: Course Website, Certificate of Achievement

This was the second course in a series of courses that I took on Apache Spark and big data. This course introduced the underlying statistical and algorithmic principles required to develop scalable real-world machine learning pipelines. The course assignments were done in PySpark to implement several scalable learning pipelines.

Image and Video Processing: From Mars to Hollywood with a Stop at the Hospital ↑

  • Institution: Duke University
  • Date Completed: June 20, 2017
  • Links: Course Website, Certificate of Achievement

In this course, we learned about the science behind how digital images and video are made, altered, stored, and used. In particular, we covered: Image and Video Compression (JPEG and MPEG), Spatial Processing (median filtering and histogram equalization), Image Restoration, Image Segmentation (graph cuts and active contours), Image and Video Inpainting (geometric PDEs), Sparse Modeling and Compressed Sensing, and Medical Imaging.

Game Theory 1↑

  • Institution: Stanford University and the University of British Columbia
  • Date Completed: November 13, 2016
  • Links: Course Website, Certificate of Achievement

This course provided an introduction to game theory. Game theory is the mathematical modeling of strategic interaction among rational (and irrational) agents. We covered the following formalization of games: Extensive-Form Games, Repeated Games, Bayesian Games, Coalitional Games. We also studied strategies and concepts such as: Pure and Mixed-Strategy Nash Equilibrium, Iterative Removal of Strictly Dominated Strategies, Minimax strategies, Subgame Perfect Equilibrium.
